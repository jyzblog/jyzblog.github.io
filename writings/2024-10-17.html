Excerpts - How to Know a Person

October 16, 2024

Chapter 1: The Power of Being Seen

This chapter emphasizes the human need to be seen and understood by others. This need is fundamental to human well-being. The author reflects on how he learned to be more present and attentive to others after a panel discussion where he realized his tendency to be detached and withdrawn. He highlights the importance of recognizing and combating common tendencies that prevent us from truly seeing others, including:

  • Egotism: Being too self-centered to genuinely listen and understand others.
  • Anxiety: Allowing social anxieties to hijack attention and prevent genuine engagement.
  • Objectivism: Treating people as objects or data points instead of complex individuals.
  • Essentialism: Making assumptions about people based on group affiliations.

The author stresses the significance of approaching others with tenderness, a quality exemplified by individuals like Mister Rogers and Ted Lasso, and emphasizes the value of seeing people's inner dignity and complexity, much like how Rembrandt portrayed his subjects. The chapter concludes by introducing Iris Murdoch's philosophy of "just and loving attention," where moral development stems from casting a selfless attention on others and seeing the world through their eyes. The author provides examples of individuals who embody this philosophy, like therapist Mary Pipher, who prioritizes understanding each patient's unique perspective and seeing them as individuals doing their best in challenging situations.

Chapter 2: How Not to See a Person

This chapter dives deeper into the barriers that hinder our ability to truly see others. It begins by highlighting the common experience of feeling stereotyped and categorized, leading to feelings of being misjudged and misunderstood. The author provides further examples of the "Diminisher tricks" that prevent us from accurately perceiving others:

  • Egotism: The inability to step outside of one's own point of view and a lack of curiosity about others.
  • Anxiety: The tendency to project one's own anxieties and insecurities onto others, leading to misinterpretations.
  • Distortion: The inclination to perceive others through the lens of one's own emotional state, leading to biased judgments.
  • Distance: The habit of objectifying others, creating a sense of separation and detachment.
  • Essentialism: The tendency to reduce individuals to group stereotypes, ignoring their unique qualities.

The chapter examines the story of Vivian Gornick and her mother, Bess, as depicted in Gornick's memoir Fierce Attachments. Their relationship illustrates how even deep love and lifelong communication can coexist with a profound inability to truly see and understand each other. Both women are locked in a blame-driven dynamic, each focused on the other's impact on themselves rather than seeking to understand the other's perspective. The author argues that this inability to see stems from their self-absorption and their focus on the effects the other has on them.

Chapter 3: Illumination

This chapter shifts the focus to the qualities that enable us to illuminate others and make them feel seen. The author underscores the importance of tenderness in seeing others, much like how Rembrandt portrayed his subjects with their flaws while simultaneously revealing their inner dignity and complexity.

The author contrasts the impersonal and abstract nature of traditional moral systems with Iris Murdoch's emphasis on "just and loving attention" as the core of moral life. Murdoch's approach prioritizes personal, concrete encounters, urging individuals to see others deeply and with discernment, resisting self-centeredness and prejudice. The chapter emphasizes that morality often manifests in small, everyday acts of consideration and connection, rather than grand gestures.

The author presents Mary Pipher, a therapist, as an exemplar of Murdoch's philosophy. Pipher emphasizes the importance of genuine, attentive conversation in therapy, viewing it as "the purest form of love." She approaches her patients with a "happy realism," acknowledging the challenges they face while emphasizing their vulnerability and capacity for love. Pipher highlights the importance of understanding the influence of family on an individual's experiences and advocates for appreciating the positive aspects of families, even in their imperfections.

Chapter 4: Accompaniment

This chapter explores the concept of accompaniment, a way of being present with others that deepens connection and trust. The author draws inspiration from Loren Eiseley's essay "The Flow of the River," where Eiseley describes the interconnectedness of nature experienced during a float down the Platte River. The author suggests that a similar attitude of openness and receptivity can guide our interactions with others, fostering a sense of shared humanity.

Accompaniment involves:

  • Deep Listening: Engaging in conversations that go beyond surface-level exchanges and truly delve into the other person's thoughts, feelings, and experiences.
  • Shared Laughter: Recognizing the power of humor to create connection and lighten the mood, particularly when dealing with challenging topics.
  • Mutual Vulnerability: Being willing to open up and share one's own experiences and vulnerabilities, fostering a sense of trust and reciprocity.
  • Compassionate Presence: Showing up for others during difficult times, offering support without judgment or the need to offer solutions.

The chapter emphasizes the importance of surrendering power in accompaniment, allowing others to take the lead in their own growth and discovery. It concludes by highlighting the transformative power of witnessing another person's journey and believing in their potential, creating a space where they feel seen and supported.

Chapter 5: What is a Person?

This chapter grapples with the question of what constitutes a person, highlighting the subjective nature of individual experience and the challenges in truly understanding another. The author uses Emmanuel Carrère's memoir Lives Other Than My Own to illustrate this concept. Carrère's account of the 2004 tsunami in Sri Lanka, where he witnessed the varying responses of individuals to the same tragic event, reveals the profound differences in how people process and experience the world.

The chapter emphasizes that understanding a person requires recognizing the two layers of reality:

  • Objective Reality: The events that occur in a person's life.
  • Subjective Reality: How those events are perceived, interpreted, and made meaningful by the individual.

The author argues that the subjective layer is often more important in understanding a person. To truly know someone, we must explore their internal landscape, inquiring about their interpretations, perspectives, and the way they construct their reality.

Chapter 6: Good Talks

This chapter focuses on the art of conversation as a crucial aspect of seeing and understanding others. The author acknowledges the common misconception that we are better conversationalists than we actually are. He presents a set of non-obvious strategies to enhance conversational skills:

  • Treat Attention as an On/Off Switch: Fully engage in the conversation, eliminating distractions and practicing active listening techniques like the SLANT method (sit up, lean forward, ask questions, nod your head, track the speaker).
  • Make Them Authors, Not Witnesses: Encourage detailed storytelling by asking specific questions that prompt vivid recollection and emotional exploration.
  • Listen to Learn, Not to Respond: Resist the urge to formulate responses while the other person is speaking. Practice pausing and reflecting before replying, creating space for deeper listening.
  • Do the Looping: Paraphrase and repeat back what the other person has said to ensure accurate understanding and encourage them to clarify their thoughts and feelings.

These strategies aim to create a conversational environment where both parties feel heard, understood, and respected. The chapter emphasizes the power of listening to foster connection, understanding, and growth.

Chapter 7: The Right Questions

This chapter delves into the importance of asking the right questions to deepen understanding and facilitate meaningful conversations. The author highlights the natural curiosity of children, who constantly ask questions to make sense of the world. He argues that adults should strive to maintain this inquisitive spirit, particularly in their interactions with others.

The chapter distinguishes between humble questions and evaluative questions:

  • Humble Questions: Open-ended questions that invite the other person to share their experiences, thoughts, and feelings without judgment or preconceptions. Examples include: "How did you...," "What's it like...," "Tell me about...," and "In what ways..."
  • Evaluative Questions: Questions that imply a right or wrong answer, often reflecting the questioner's biases and limiting the conversation.

The author stresses the need for openness and curiosity when asking questions, allowing the conversation to unfold organically and creating a space for the other person to feel comfortable sharing their perspective. He cites research demonstrating the human need to share information about themselves, even negative experiences, and how feeling listened to can reduce loneliness and enhance well-being.

Chapter 8: The Epidemic of Blindness

This chapter explores the societal context of disconnection and distrust that makes it challenging to see and understand others. The author cites rising rates of depression, anxiety, and suicide as evidence of a growing crisis of loneliness and isolation. He argues that this epidemic of blindness stems from a culture that prioritizes individual achievement and self-promotion over empathy and connection.

The chapter examines instances of historical and contemporary conflicts, highlighting how group identities and power dynamics can lead to dehumanization and violence. It emphasizes the importance of acknowledging the influence of social structures and historical legacies on individual experiences and the need to approach conversations across differences with sensitivity and awareness.

Chapter 9: Hard Conversations

This chapter focuses on the challenges and strategies involved in having "hard conversations" - conversations that occur across differences and perceived power inequalities. The author draws from his own experiences and insights from experts to offer guidance on navigating these complex interactions.

He stresses the importance of considering the conditions in which the conversation takes place, recognizing the potential impact of social dynamics and power imbalances. He encourages individuals to approach these conversations with genuine curiosity about the other person's experiences and perspectives, particularly if they come from a different background or hold different beliefs.

The chapter highlights the two levels on which every conversation operates:

  • Official Conversation: The surface-level exchange of words and ideas about the nominal topic being discussed.
  • Actual Conversation: The underlying current of emotions, intentions, and power dynamics that shapes the interaction.

The author argues that attending to the "actual conversation" is crucial for success. He emphasizes the importance of creating a safe space where both parties feel respected and heard, even when discussing sensitive or controversial issues. He advises against:

  • Yanking the conversation back to your frame: Resist the temptation to dominate the conversation or dismiss the other person's experiences.
  • Failing to step into the other person's ken: Make an effort to understand their world view and experiences.
  • Creating an us/them dynamic: Strive to find common ground and build a shared pool of knowledge.

The chapter concludes by emphasizing that hard conversations are hard because people from different backgrounds construct different realities, leading to misunderstandings and conflicts. The author cites research on perception to demonstrate how individual experiences shape our understanding of the world and contribute to these differences.

Chapter 10: How Do You Serve a Friend Who Is in Despair?

This chapter shifts the focus to supporting friends and loved ones struggling with depression, grief, and despair. The author shares his personal experience with a close friend, Pete, who battled depression and ultimately took his own life. He reflects on the challenges and lessons learned from accompanying Pete during this difficult period.

The author emphasizes the importance of:

  • Presence over Solutions: Being present and supportive without feeling the need to offer solutions or advice.
  • Validation and Empathy: Acknowledging and validating the person's experiences and feelings without judgment or dismissal.
  • Small Touches and Reminders: Offering consistent support through small gestures like notes, emails, or visits to show care and concern.
  • Patience and Understanding: Recognizing that depression is a complex illness and recovery is a journey, not a destination.

The author acknowledges the limitations of introspection in dealing with depression, highlighting the benefits of seeking support from others, including therapists and friends. He stresses the importance of creating a space where the person feels safe to share their experiences without shame or judgment.

Chapter 11: The Art of Empathy

This chapter explores empathy as a crucial skill for understanding and connecting with others, particularly those who are struggling. The author challenges the traditional view of emotions as disruptive forces, arguing instead that they contain valuable information and guide us through life. He emphasizes the importance of understanding not only what others think but also how they feel.

The chapter highlights the role of mirroring in empathy, where individuals unconsciously mimic the facial expressions, body language, and tone of voice of those they interact with. This process helps to build rapport and facilitates emotional understanding.

The author discusses the concept of emotional granularity, the ability to identify and articulate a wide range of emotions. He argues that individuals with high emotional granularity are better equipped to understand and respond to the emotional experiences of others. Conversely, those who struggle to differentiate between emotions may have difficulty empathizing with others.

The chapter explores the challenges of empathizing with individuals who exhibit low empathy themselves, such as those with borderline personality disorder. The author acknowledges that empathy can be emotionally taxing, particularly for those who are highly sensitive to the emotions of others. However, he emphasizes the transformative power of empathy in building deep, meaningful connections and fostering compassion.

Chapter 12: How Were You Shaped by Your Sufferings?

This chapter examines the impact of suffering and loss on individual identity and the process of meaning-making that follows. The author uses the example of Barbara Lazear Ascher, who faced the impending death of her husband from pancreatic cancer. Ascher's memoir Ghosting details the transformative experience of confronting mortality, highlighting the importance of facing grief and finding meaning in loss.

The author argues that understanding someone who has experienced significant suffering requires knowing how they have processed their loss and whether they have emerged stronger or broken. He presents a framework for understanding the journey of grief and growth:

  • Initial Shock and Numbness: The immediate aftermath of loss, where emotions may be overwhelming and difficult to process.
  • Emotional Excavation: A gradual process of confronting and working through the emotions and experiences associated with the loss.
  • Meaning-Making and Growth: Integrating the loss into one's life story and finding new meaning and purpose in the face of adversity.

The author emphasizes the importance of sharing one's story with trusted individuals as a crucial step in processing grief and finding healing. He offers several exercises to facilitate this process:

  • Story-Prompting Questions: Asking open-ended questions that encourage reflection and storytelling.
  • "This Is Your Life": Writing a summary of the year from the other person's perspective, focusing on their challenges and triumphs.
  • "Filling in the Calendar": Walking through significant periods of the person's life, year by year, to stimulate memories and reflection.
  • Story Sampling: Engaging in free-form expressive writing exercises to explore emotions and experiences in a safe and private space.
  • Conversations with Friends: Sharing stories and reflections on the loss with trusted individuals.

The author argues that by sharing their stories and reinterpreting their experiences, individuals can create new mental models and construct a more resilient and meaningful future.

Chapter 13: Personality: What Energy Do You Bring into the Room?

This chapter explores the concept of personality and its role in shaping how individuals interact with the world. The author discusses the Big Five personality traits - openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, and neuroticism - as a widely accepted framework for understanding personality. He provides detailed explanations of each trait, including their potential strengths and weaknesses, and offers examples of how these traits manifest in everyday life.

The author emphasizes the importance of understanding personality traits not only for self-awareness but also for effectively interacting with others. He argues that recognizing and appreciating individual differences in personality can improve communication, relationships, and parenting. He also discusses the potential pitfalls of taking certain traits to extremes, using the example of someone with excessive conscientiousness who becomes rigid and controlling.

Chapter 14: Life Tasks

This chapter presents a developmental psychology perspective on understanding individuals through the lens of "life tasks" - common challenges and transitions that people face throughout their lives. The author emphasizes that these tasks are not fixed stages but rather represent common patterns of human behavior and development.

He outlines several common life tasks and the corresponding states of consciousness that individuals develop to navigate them:

  • Establishing a Sense of Agency (Imperial Consciousness): This early task involves developing a sense of self and independence, often characterized by self-centeredness and a focus on personal desires.
  • Forming Interpersonal Relationships: This task involves learning to connect with others and navigate the complexities of relationships, often accompanied by a shift from an egocentric perspective to a more interpersonal one.
  • Career Consolidation: This task involves establishing a stable career path and identity, often characterized by a focus on achievement and professional success.
  • Generativity: This midlife task involves contributing to something larger than oneself, often through parenting, mentorship, or community involvement.
  • Integrity and Wisdom: This later-life task involves reflecting on one's life and finding meaning and acceptance, often characterized by a shift from a focus on achievement to a focus on relationships and legacy.

The author emphasizes that individuals may approach these tasks in different orders and that not everyone completes all of them. However, recognizing the life tasks that someone is grappling with can provide valuable insights into their motivations, behaviors, and perspectives.

Chapter 15: Life Stories

This chapter explores the significance of personal narratives in understanding individual identity and meaning-making. The author draws from the work of psychologist Dan McAdams, who studies how people construct and tell the stories of their lives. McAdams' research suggests that individuals find great satisfaction in sharing their life stories and that doing so can be a deeply meaningful and therapeutic experience.

The author distinguishes between two modes of thinking:

  • Paradigmatic Mode: Analytical and argument-driven thinking, often employed in professional settings.
  • Narrative Mode: Storytelling and meaning-making thinking, crucial for understanding individuals and their experiences.

The author advocates for "storifying" life, shifting from a focus on data and analysis to a focus on personal narratives. He argues that crafting a coherent life story is essential for developing a stable identity, finding meaning in experiences, and navigating life's challenges.

When listening to someone's life story, the author suggests paying attention to:

  • Narrative Tone: The characteristic voice and attitude conveyed in the story, revealing the person's outlook on the world and their sense of self-efficacy.
  • Overarching Plotline: The dominant theme or narrative arc that shapes the person's understanding of their life, often drawing from common cultural narratives.
  • Narrative Flexibility: The ability to adapt and revise one's life story in response to new experiences and challenges.

The author highlights the importance of recognizing the potential for self-deception and distortion in personal narratives. He cautions against overly perfect or evasive stories that lack authenticity and vulnerability. He also emphasizes that listening to someone's story can be a powerful act of affirmation and support, helping them to make sense of their experiences and shape their identity.

Chapter 16: How Do Your Ancestors Show Up in Your Life?

This chapter examines the influence of culture and ancestry on individual identity and behavior. The author uses the life of Zora Neale Hurston, a prominent writer and anthropologist, to illustrate how cultural heritage can shape a person's worldview and aspirations. Hurston's upbringing in Eatonville, Florida, an all-Black town, instilled in her a sense of pride and self-reliance that influenced her work and her life.

The author discusses research on cultural psychology, highlighting the differences in thinking styles and values across cultures. He cites the work of Joseph Henrich, who argues that Western, educated, industrialized, rich, and democratic (WEIRD) societies are outliers in terms of their individualistic and analytical thinking styles. Henrich suggests that cultural practices, such as rice farming, can influence cognitive styles and social norms.

The author emphasizes the importance of recognizing the interplay between individual agency and cultural influence, urging readers to see individuals as both shaped by their heritage and capable of forging their own paths. He encourages a "double vision" that appreciates the power of cultural forces while simultaneously recognizing the unique experiences and perspectives of each person.

Chapter 17: What is Wisdom?

This chapter redefines wisdom as a practical skill rooted in understanding and empathy rather than an abstract intellectual pursuit. The author challenges the traditional view of the wise person as a distant sage dispensing pronouncements, arguing instead that true wisdom lies in the ability to see deeply into the lives and experiences of others.

He presents examples of wise individuals who:

  • Witness the Story: Listen attentively and empathetically to the experiences and struggles of others.
  • Help Process Thoughts and Emotions: Provide a safe space for individuals to explore their inner world and navigate complex feelings.
  • Focus on Relationships: Emphasize the importance of nurturing and improving interpersonal connections as a source of meaning and support.
  • See Gifts and Potential: Recognize and affirm the strengths and possibilities within individuals, even those they may not see themselves.

The author emphasizes that wisdom stems from lived experience, reflection, and deep engagement with others. It is a product of navigating life's challenges, joys, and sorrows with compassion and understanding. He encourages readers to cultivate wisdom by embracing experiences, reflecting on their meaning, and seeking connection with others.

The chapter concludes with several case studies illustrating the transformative power of being seen and understood by others:

  • Tracy Kidder's Strength in What Remains: This book tells the story of Deo, a Burundian refugee who endured immense hardship and found healing and purpose through the support of compassionate individuals.
  • Lori Gottlieb's Maybe You Should Talk to Someone: This book describes Gottlieb's experience as a therapist working with John, a narcissistic and self-absorbed television writer who undergoes a profound transformation through therapy.
  • The film Good Will Hunting: This film depicts the relationship between Will Hunting, a troubled genius, and his therapist, Sean, who challenges and supports Will to confront his past and embrace his potential.

These examples demonstrate how wisdom in action involves:

  • Critiquing with Care: Offering honest feedback and challenges within a context of support and empathy.
  • Establishing Trust and Connection: Creating a safe space where individuals feel comfortable being vulnerable and sharing their experiences.
  • Recognizing Potential and Encouraging Growth: Helping individuals to see their own strengths and possibilities and supporting them in pursuing their aspirations.

The book ends with a call to action, urging readers to embrace the challenge of becoming Illuminators - individuals who see others deeply, cultivate wisdom, and contribute to a more connected and compassionate world.
